Blog: What one AI email actually costs

Most AI ROI talk is hand-waving. So I wrote up a traced, per-email cost from a production agent.

The number, end to end: about $0.03 per email, in roughly 20 seconds, for a sourced reply grounded in product documentation. Versus a knowledgeable person at a $40/hour loaded rate, the same lookup-and-reply task is around $5 and the customer waits hours. At 1,000 emails a month, that is ~$30 vs ~$5,000 — more than a 100x difference, and hours-to-seconds in speed.

The point is not "replace people." A category of repetitive, lookup-heavy email stops consuming a sizable chunk of skilled time each month and gets answered instantly — freeing that person for work that actually needs them.

The real risk is not cost. It is the agent confidently inventing a spec, a price, or a model number and sending it to a customer over your name. So the engineering discipline is the part most pilots skip: ground every answer in a source, cite the file it came from, and decline rather than improvise when the question is out of scope. In a recent run of 29 inbound emails, every one of the 5 out-of-scope questions was correctly declined — no fabricated specs.

The post also separates operating cost (~$0.03 per email) from build cost — the one-time engineering investment of retrieval, grounding, guardrails, observability, scale-to-zero deploy. An ROI number that hides the build cost is exactly the kind of claim the AI hype cycle has earned its skepticism for.

Numbers read straight from OpenTelemetry traces in Logfire — checkable, not asserted.
